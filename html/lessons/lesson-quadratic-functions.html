Scene 1

Start with the vibe: smile or frown

y = ax²

Before numbers, formulas, or panic: a quadratic in its simplest form is “How does this curve feel?”

  • a > 0 → opens up → U-shape → “bowl”, “minimum”, “holding things”.
  • a < 0 → opens down → ∩-shape → “hill”, “maximum”, “about to fall”.
  • No need to solve anything. Just check the sign of a.

Quick check: For y = -2x², is this a bowl or a hill?

Scene 2

Where’s the turning point?

y = a(x − h)² + k

Imaging tossing a ball upward. It slows, stops, and then falls - that pause in motion is the vertex of its path. On a graph, that's the highest or lowest point of a parabola.

  • a>0, your ball starts at the ground and goes up then down (a minimum point).
  • a<0, it’s like tossing the ball from above — it falls and bounces up (a maximum point).

Quick check: For y = 3(x - 1)² - 4, what’s the vertex?

Scene 3

How dramatic is the curve?

“a” controls intensity

  • |a| small (e.g. 0.5) → wide, chill curve.
  • |a| big (e.g. 3) → narrow, dramatic spike.
  • Sign of a: up vs down. Size of a: how “extra” the curve is.

Watch how changing a alone reshapes the same vertex.

Scene 4

Roots = where the story hits the ground

Roots are x where y = 0

On the graph, roots are just where the curve crosses the x-axis. On paper, those are the solutions of the quadratic equation.

  • 2 roots → curve cuts the axis twice.
  • 1 root → curve just kisses the axis (touches at the vertex).
  • 0 roots → curve floats completely above/below the axis.

Quick check: Based on the picture above, do you expect 2, 1, or 0 real roots?

Scene 5

Why this matters for exams

Standard form: y = ax² + bx + c

Exam questions love this version. But under the hood, it’s the same curve you’ve just met:

  • You can rewrite to vertex form to find the turning point faster.
  • You can factor (if nice) to see roots immediately.
  • The graph is not new. It’s the same character in a different outfit.

Checkpoint: For y = 2x² + 4x + 1, which move helps you see the vertex?

Unlocked: graph sense for quadratics

If you can look at any quadratic and quickly say: “up/down, where’s the vertex, how many roots, wide or narrow?” — you’re ready for solving, completing the square, and formula questions.
