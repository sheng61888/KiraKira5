Module 02 · Quadratic Equations

Finding where the ball hits the ground

We’ve met the parabola — now let’s figure out when it lands.

Scene 1

The Two Moments of Impact

y = ax² + bx + c

The parabola tells a story of motion — the ball rises, falls, and hits the ground. Each place it touches the ground represents a solution to the equation where y = 0.

  • Solving ax² + bx + c = 0 means finding the x-values where the height becomes 0.
  • Those x-values are called the roots or solutions.

Quick check: For y = x² − 6x + 8, how many times does the ball hit the ground?

Scene 2

Factoring — Finding the Ground Touch Points

y = (x − p)(x − q)

If we can rewrite a quadratic into two simple brackets, we immediately know where the ball lands. Each bracket gives one impact point: (x − root) = 0.

Quick check: Which equation lands at x = 2 and x = 4?

Scene 3

Completing the Square — Recenter the Path

y = (x + p)² + q

Sometimes it’s easier to see the parabola’s shape by “recentering” it at its vertex. Completing the square helps us rewrite ax² + bx + c into this form.

  • It shows where the vertex is — the ball’s highest or lowest point.
  • It helps us visualize symmetry and calculate roots more easily.

Quick check: What’s the vertex form of x² + 4x + 1?

Scene 4

The Universal Impact Finder

x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a

This formula works for any quadratic. The expression under the square root — b² − 4ac — tells us how many real impacts the ball makes.

  • If b² − 4ac > 0 → two real roots.
  • If b² − 4ac = 0 → one repeated root.
  • If b² − 4ac < 0 → no real roots (the ball never reaches the ground).

Quick check: If b² − 4ac = 0, how many times does the ball touch the ground?

Unlocked: Solving the full motion

You’ve seen how every method tells the same story — whether through factoring, completing the square, or the formula, the goal is always the same: find where the curve meets the ground.
